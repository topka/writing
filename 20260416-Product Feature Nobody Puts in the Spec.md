# Product Feature Nobody Puts in the Spec

Like many, I was playing with OpenClaw recently. Well, “playing” is a word — I was actually elbow-deep in it. It was resisting, I was insisting, failing and then succeeding. Was I happy when it worked in the end? Oh, boy, happy does not even describe it.

That feeling is not an accident. It’s a design outcome. My bet is most teams just don’t know they’re choosing it.

Two products, same category, roughly similar quality. One has users who evangelize, customize, and never leave. The other churns at normal rates, runs decent NPS, and wonders why its community programs never quite take off.

The difference usually isn’t features.

When users configure a product — really tinker with it, bend it to their workflow, build inside it something that didn’t exist before — something shifts. The product stops being a vendor’s product and starts being theirs. That psychological shift is the actual retention mechanism. Not the feature set. Not the onboarding flow.

Behavioral economists call this the IKEA effect: things we partially assemble, we value more than things handed to us complete. The same dynamic runs through software, often invisibly and often unplanned.

Android users who’ve spent a weekend customizing their launcher don’t switch to iPhone because switching would mean losing something *they built*. Notion users talk about “my setup” in a way Confluence users rarely do. People who’ve configured Home Assistant — an open-source home automation platform that borders on hostile to newcomers — speak about their system the way people speak about a house they renovated themselves. They own it, because they made it. I’m talking from my own experience here.

The switching cost here isn’t data migration. It’s the loss of something you created.

This dynamic almost never shows up in a product spec. Teams think carefully about UX quality, onboarding friction, time-to-value. Very few ask, early and deliberately: *how much of this product will users feel they built?*

That question belongs in the design conversation. It isn’t there.

For product marketers, the gap is just as real. Marketing for configurable products almost always leads with power and flexibility. It rarely names the actual emotional outcome — *this will feel like yours*. That’s harder to show in a demo video. It’s also the reason users stay loyal and can’t stop telling others.

The most uncomfortable version of this problem shows up later, when teams try to fix it.

Ambassador programs. Power user communities. “Build with us” campaigns. These initiatives make sense — they’re designed to create exactly the kind of deep, vocal loyalty that configurable products seem to generate naturally. But they routinely underperform on products that were never designed for ownership in the first place.

The issue isn’t execution. It’s that you can’t retrofit ownership culture onto a product that never gave users anything to own. If the product is fully polished, fully decided, fully controlled by the vendor — the user’s role is to consume it, not build with it. No community program changes that underlying relationship.

The practical implication is simple but easy to miss: the ownership question is a design decision, and it needs to be made early. Not because every product should be open and configurable — polished and opinionated products have real advantages, and plenty of users prefer them. But because the choice shapes everything downstream.

Your GTM, your community strategy, your retention model, your word-of-mouth assumptions — all of them rest on a bet about how much users will feel this product is theirs. Most teams make that choice by accident. In a product spec meeting. Long before anyone from marketing is in the room.

That spec meeting is a marketing meeting. Nobody sent the invite.
