# Marketing's Inconvenient Truth

I just watched a DVJ Insights' 2026 edition of their Brand Growth Event based on the research they run for 10 years.

The highlight was Mark Ritson's presentation of 10 top drivers of marketing growth.  
 And it looks like Mark has updated his list from what I remember.  
 And not a small update either, there's a new number one — the **category you're in**.  
 Number two is brand size. Number three — the product.

Let's think about that for a moment.

The three biggest drivers of growth are things most marketers have little to no control over. Category is usually decided before the marketing team exists or before you joined the team. Brand size is the accumulated result of years — sometimes decades — of decisions, many of them not marketing's. Product, with rare exceptions, belongs to someone else's roadmap.

I do not want to criticize Ritson's framework. It's the opposite. It's one of the most clarifying things I've heard in a while.

Categories are also not permanent. They grow, plateau, and die. You can execute flawlessly inside a dying category and still lose (Kodak anyone?).

So what should I or you as a marketer actually do with this?

First, accept it. The structural game is largely set before you arrive. Fighting that reality wastes energy that could go elsewhere, somewhere where your effort will matter.

Second, know your conditions. The drivers lower on Ritson's list — strategy, creativity, media investment, pricing — are where marketers have real agency. Which of those actually apply to your situation? Start there.

Marketing (being marketing) often oversells its own power and influence over structural outcomes. That sets everyone up for disappointment — leaders, teams, and the business. The more honest path is to understand what you can move, focus hard on that, and resist the pressure to pretend that everything is in your hands.

And trust me, that's not pessimism. That's focus and focus is how you actually help.
