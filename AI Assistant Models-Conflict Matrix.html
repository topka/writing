How to read this matrix: Columns C1–C7 evaluate from the employee's perspective (how well does this model protect the individual's interests?). Columns O1–O4 evaluate from the organization's perspective (how well does this model serve the company's interests?). Columns V1–V3 evaluate practical viability (how complex, usable, and buildable is this model?). The right model is the one that finds an acceptable balance across all three perspectives — not the one that maximizes any single dimension.
Key observation: Models that score well for the employee (M4) tend to score poorly for the organization, and vice versa (M1, M5). M8 offers maximum protection but at a UX cost that undermines the employee's actual interests. M6 emerges as the most balanced model — strong for the employee, adequate for the org, and viable with existing protocol standards. M7 adds AI-mediated coordination for a better UX but at higher implementation cost. The design question is whether M6's user-managed coordination is sufficient or whether the market will demand M7's automation.
