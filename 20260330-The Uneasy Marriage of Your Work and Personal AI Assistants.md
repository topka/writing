# The Uneasy Marriage of Your Work and Personal AI Assistants

Have you ever had a personal assistant (PA) at your company or as an individual?   
Chances are that most of you reading this — have not.  
I predict however that with the current advancements of agentic AI, we may soon *all* get personal assistants, virtual ones. The pioneers are already running [OpenClaw](https://openclaw.ai/) or similar agents and claim a great boost in productivity or freed time, or both. Yes, specialized agents can really help in automating routines cheaply and quite efficiently.

But what I want to touch here is a couple of specific aspects of personal AI assistants: 

1. **Multiple contexts**: at least personal and work contexts  
2. **Conflicts of interest** between the parties, namely the individual and the organization they work at.

Now, imagine you have a nimble PA helping you by unloading various routine work related tasks from you, like managing email, calendars and documents. Lucky you\!  
Wouldn’t you want such a PA for your personal life? I mean we all have personal goals, responsibilities, and then calendars and appointments. A truly personal assistant seems tempting.

And here we are — we have our two **contexts**. 

It’s nothing new, we the working individuals are dealing with them constantly. We plan family vacations (both contexts mixed), we go on business trips (focus on work aligning with personal), or find time for our doctor appointment or children’s cello concert (focus on personal). We have all the data and resolve those time and priority conflicts between these contexts using our own judgement.

What’s the problem? Let’s share access to your personal calendar, email and other data with your work-related *virtual* assistant\! Sounds quite logical and simple at first glance.

But how much do you trust that agent? Whose interests does it pursue? Do you share everything with it or just limited parts and neutral “personal commitments” in the calendar? While you are thinking, let me give you a couple of spicy examples:

* You receive a potential job offer email from a competitor company or you are interviewing with other companies  
* You need to plan a medical treatment that will affect availability at work  
* You are planning to take extended leave

In each case, you are interested in information protection. And in each case your organization's interest — even if not malicious — is to have access to any information that affects workforce planning.

And so we have the **conflict of interest**.

And the conflict is not only in information access, it also exists in decision-making principles (aligning priorities), and, very importantly, in data custody. Where does the memory of each assistant live? Who controls the data after you leave a job? What data is retained by your personal assistant and you as a result? 

These areas create a number of downstream questions about how the system should be designed. And that's before we even consider whose platform these assistants run on.

The reason this problem is interesting isn't the technology. We already know how to build agents that manage calendars and filter email. The hard part is that the two sides of your life have legitimately competing claims on the same information, and no single system can serve both loyalties without compromising one. That's a design problem, not an engineering one. And it's the design problem I'll dig into next.
